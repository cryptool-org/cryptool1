e is prime relative to phi(N)

This is an important condition for the public key of the RSA method.

It is said that e is relatively prime to phi(N) when e and phi(N) are co-prime, i.e. they do not possess any common divisor (apart from the number one). This condition is a requirement that the number e possesses a multiplicative inverse d modulus phi(N).

Let a and m be natural numbers and gcd(a,m) the greatest common divisor of a and m. The following is then true:

For all a and m there exist whole numbers x,y so that gcd(a,m) = x*a + y*m.

The extended Euclidean algorithm calculates these numbers x and y. When gcd(e,phi(N)) = 1 then it follows that

x*e + phi(N)*y = 1,

and when the equation modulus phi(N) is calculated

x*e = 1 - phi(N)*y = 1 (mod phi(N)).

The number x is thus the multiplicatively inverse number modulus phi(N). During the RSA key generation procedure, the extended Euclidean algorithm calculates the secret exponent d = x for RSA decryption.


Euler´s totient function phi(N) of a positive integer N is defined to be the number of positive integers less than or equal to n and coprime to n.

Example: phi(8) = 4, since the four numbers 1, 3, 5, 7 are coprime to 8.
