There are many cryptographic challenges and puzzles published. Here are some more well know.

Particularly exciting is the method used in number 5, the double-column transposition:

Despite the original double-column transposition challenge (from Klaus Schmeh) was solved by Gorge Lasry in 2013 it still holds: If used "correctly", this historic cipher even resists modern attacks because it roughly has the same resistibility (key space) as RC5.

The double-column transposition is an encryption algorithm that can be executed by hand without the help of a computer and is still considered quite safe. The double-column transposition was (and probably still is) used by spies who did not want to arouse suspicion by using an encryption device or a computer program.

Despite of its wide usage, till 1999 only a few scientific analyses about the double-column transposition were known. Thus, the security of the algorithm could hardly be estimated. For this reason, Otto Leiberich, former president of the German Information Security Agency (BSI), suggested in 1999 in an article published several times [2] to create a cryptographic riddle, which deals with solving a double-column transposition ciphertext.

Klaus Schmeh adopted this idea and encrypted an English text with two passwords of different length and published the ciphertext in his book "Codebreaker vs. Codemaker" [1], in a Telepolis article [3], and as MTC3 challenge
https://www.mysterytwisterc3.org/en/challenges/level-x/double-column-transposition.
Also see http://scienceblogs.de/klausis-krypto-kolumne/2013/09/13/top-25-der-ungelosten-verschlusselungen-platz-5-die-doppelwurfel-challenge/ .

After 6 years, where despite many efforts no breakthrough seems to come, the original challenge was solved by George Lasry [4].

In order to show that double-column transposition is still secure today when using "correct" parameters, end of 2013 the series "Double Column Transposition Reloaded" was created:
https://www.mysterytwisterc3.org/en/challenges/level-iii/double-column-transposition-reloaded-part-1

2) Further cipher challenges / contests (mostly mathematically oriented)

  • From Simon Singh's "The Code Book": solved,
    see http://simonsingh.net/cryptography/
  • RSA cipher or RSA factoring challenges (started in 1991, ended in 2007): This is the most well-known cipher contest. For some of the challenges prize money was advertised for the decryption.
    See http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSA_Factoring_Challenge
  • RSA Secret-Key Challenge
    see http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSA_Secret-Key_Challenge
  • Certicom (RIM) ECC Challenge 1997
    see http://www.certicom.com/ and https://www.certicom.com/index.php/the-certicom-ecc-challenge
  • Applied Crypto Challenge Problems (Mark Stamp)
    see http://www.cs.sjsu.edu/~stamp/crypto/ChallengeProblems/challengeProblems.html
  • Number Shark or Taxman Challenge
    This teaching game was implemented in CT1 and JCT. Additionally the authors investigated a bit the mathematical borders for finding the optimal integer sequence https://oeis.org/A019312.
  • TU Darmstadt Lattice Challenge 2008
    see http://www.latticechallenge.org
  • Mystery Twister started in 2005 and was a large-scale contest of the university of Bochum. It could not be finished due to the passing of Hans Dobbertin and missing sponsors. This challenge was revived from 2010 in a new form together with the CrypTool team. Since then it is one of the most active and diversified contests of this kind: MysteryTwister C3.
