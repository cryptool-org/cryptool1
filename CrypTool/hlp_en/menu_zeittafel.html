Time-table / time-travel through cryptography

This overview of the various cryptographic algorithms is a chronological history of cryptology, whose origins date back to the days of antiquity and which over time has undergone steady refinement of its methods. Please note that this table is by no means complete.

The first human writing dates back more than 6000 years. Encryption has been in existence for around 3000 years.

c. 1900 BC Non standard characters.
c. 1000 BC Mesopotamia.
c. 600 BC In Palestine, texts were encrypted with Atbash.
c. 500 BC The Greeks encrypted messages using Skytale.
c. 200 BC The Greek historian Polybius described his Polybius system for the first time.
c. 100-44 BC Julius Caesar wrote confidential messages in the code named after him, the Caesar code. This is the most well-known of all the mono-alphabetic algorithms.
c. 500-1400 AD The "dark age of cryptography" in Europe: during this period cryptography was assigned to black magic and a lot of knowledge regarding cryptography was lost. By contrast cryptography flourished in the Persian world.
855 AD In the Arabic world the first book on cryptology appeared. Among other things, Abu ‘Abd al-Raham al-Khahil ibn Ahmad ibn‚Amr ibn Tammam al Farahidi al-Zadi al Yahamadi described proudly in his book the successful decryption of a Greek ciphertext that was intended for the Byzantine Emperor.
1412 A 14-volume Arabic encyclopaedia also described cryptographic methods. Here, in addition to substitution and transposition, the first mention occurred of the method of repeated substitution applied to a plaintext character.
1397 At the request of Clement VII, Gabrieli di Lavinde wrote the first manual on cryptology (Nomenclature Code). Due to its simplicity this nomenclature code was used over the next 450 years especially in diplomatic circles.
1466 Leon Battista Alberti (1404-1472), one of the leading figures of the Italian Renaissance, published his book "Modus scribendi in ziferas", in which the cipher wheels invented by him was mentioned for the first time. Alberti‚s numerous cryptologic achievements stem from his role as secretary of an official body concerned with cryptographs at the Papal Court in Rome. He is known as the "father of cryptography".
1518 The first printed book on cryptology, written by Johannes Trithemius, appeared in the German-speaking world.
1586 The book "Tractié de Chiffre" by the French diplomat, Blaise de Vigenère, appeared. His encryption method, which was later named the Vigenère code after him, thus became public knowledge. This code is the most well-known of all the poly-alphabetic algorithms.
1628 Antoine Rissignol became the first full-time cryptanalyst to be employed after his decryption of an enemy coded message ended the siege of Realmont by the Huguenots. Since then, cryptanalysts have been a fixed element in military organisations.
1700 The Russian tsar used a big code table of 2000-3000 syllables and words to encrypt his messages.
1795 Thomas Jefferson developed the first cylindrical cipher device, known as the "wheel cipher". However, he never used it, so that it was forgotten, or rather never became public knowledge. This meant that cylindrical cipher devices were reinvented in a number of places in parallel to Jefferson‚s unannounced invention.
1854 The Englishman Charles Babbage invented a cylindrical cipher device, similar to the "wheel cipher".
1854 The English physicist, Charles Wheatstone, invented a cipher which worked with a 5*5 matrix. His friend, Lord Lyon Playfair, Baron of St. Andrews, made this public in higher military and diplomatic circles in Victorian England, and thus it came to be known as the Playfair code.
1857 Beauforts cipher
1860 Friedrich Kasiski and William F. Friedman developed statistical methods of cryptoanalysis that were able to decrypt the Vigenère code, which had up to then been unbreakable.
1883 "La Cryptographie militaire" by Auguste Kerckhoff von Nieuwendhoff appeared. This constitutes a milestone in the cryptography of the telegraph era. Contains the "principles of Kerckhoff" for strategic cryptology.
1891 The Frenchman Major Etienne Bazeries invented a cylindrical device known as the Bazeries cylinder that was similar in principle to the wheel cipher.
1917 The American, Gilbert S. Vernam, discovered and developed the one-time-pad.
1919 Magelin machines
1921 The Californian Edward Hebern built the first cipher machine based on the rotor principle.
1922 Thomas Jefferson‚s wheel cipher was discovered in the USA, further developed by the US Marines and thus came to be used during the Second World War.
1923 The Enigma rotor machine developed by the German engineer, Arthur Scherbius, was unveiled at the international post congress. The Chiffriermaschinen AG company was founded by A. Scherbius to market his Enigma around the world.
1929 Hill cipher
1941 Decoding of the Japanese attack message for the Second World War (many historians believe that cryptology saved a year of war during the Second World War).
1949 Shannon
1973 La Padula
1975 Diffie and Hellman demonstrated that public key procedures are theoretically possible, although they in fact set out to prove the opposite.
1976 DH key exchange.
1977 The DES (Data Encryption Standard) invented by IBM in 1975 was chosen as the standard cryptographic algorithm.
1978 The RSA algorithm, named after its developers, Ronald Rivest, Adi Shamir and Leonard Adleman, was published. It was the first public key procedure that can be used in practice and it ranks as the most innovative contribution of cryptologic research in the 20th-century.
1985 Goldwasser, Micali and Racoff unveiled the Zero-Knowledge procedures.
1986 Independently of each other, Neal Koblitz and Victor Miller proposed using elliptic curves in the area of public key cryptography.
1990 Xueija Lai and James Massey developed the IDEA algorithm, which, for example, is used in the PGP (Pretty Good Privacy) cryptology software developed by Phil Zimmermann since 1991.
1991 DSA, introduced by NIST.
1997 Known plaintext attack on DES successfully applied by EFF.
10/2000 After a long public competition, the algorithm developed by Rijndael was chosen by NIST as the successor of DES and is now called AES (Advanced Encryption Standard).
